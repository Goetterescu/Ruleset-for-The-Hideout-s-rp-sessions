Emis Guideline

The fire department and emergency medical services share the same guideline, they will be addressed as Emis.
  • §1
    Vehicles of the Emis are only considered as such if they meet the following requirements.
    • 1
      Emi vehicles have to be equipped with their specific lighting.
    • 2
      The driver has to be on duty as an EMS member or firefighter (use the respective command).
    • 3
      The driver or any of their passengers have to be either responding or part of an emergency. Trainings are not an exception, so drivers are not allowed to make use of this section during a simulation or similar.
  • §2
    The parking section can be ignored during an emergency.
  • §3
    There are four official codes to which any Emi can respond. These have to be forwarded to the chief of the corresponding department before the driver merges into a public road, turns on his lights/horns, or takes advantage of the codes below.
    • §3.1
      If there's no supervisor or similar personal online, the call forwarding can be ignored.
    • §3.2
      When the driver is free-roaming (responding to no call), they will be taken as regular traffic with no special rights and will have to take full responsibility when causing an accident.
      • Code 0
        Training or other similar activities. No special lights or horns are allowed; No special rights are reserved for this code.
      • Code 1
        Calls where one or more people are in the process of dying or close to that. All horns and special lights are allowed. In case of a traffic incident, the driver of the emergency vehicle can not be sued and will not have to pay for any damage.
      • Code 2
        People are in direct danger or critical health. Same rights as above but drivers of Emis can be charged in case of an accident.
      • Code 3
        General calls. No horns, but special lights are allowed. Drivers have to drive cautiously and avoid excessive speeding. Drivers of Emis can be charged in case of an accident.
  • §4
    Emi drivers have to make their intentions clear to other drivers when they're responding to a call. This includes using their sirens before approaching an intersection and giving other drivers time to react to the emergency vehicle.
